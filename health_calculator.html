Body Fat Percentage

Body Fat Percentage estimates how much of a person's body weight is made up of fat versus lean mass (muscles, bones, organs, and water). In this project, we use the U.S. Navy Method, which measures height, neck, waist, and hip (for females). It is simple, inexpensive, and fairly accurate for general fitness.

Why it is useful: Provides a better understanding of body composition than weight alone. Two people with the same weight may have very different body fat levels depending on muscle mass.

If results are high: May indicate increased risk of heart disease, diabetes, and reduced metabolic efficiency. Improvement methods include regular exercise, strength training, balanced nutrition, and maintaining caloric balance.

Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI is a widely used method to estimate whether a person's weight is appropriate for their height. It is calculated as weight (kg) divided by height (m) squared.

Why BMI is useful: Quick and easy to calculate; requires only height and weight; helps identify potential health risks in large populations.

Limitations: BMI does not differentiate between muscle and fat. Very muscular individuals, like bodybuilders or heavyweight fighters, may be classified as overweight or obese even with low body fat.

Waist-to-Hip Ratio (WHR)

WHR measures fat distribution by comparing waist and hip measurements. It is particularly useful for identifying central obesity, which refers to excess abdominal fat.

Why WHR is useful: Abdominal fat is strongly linked to heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome. Even people with normal BMI may have high abdominal fat, increasing health risks.

Using BMI, Body Fat %, and WHR together gives a complete picture of health: BMI for general screening, body fat percentage for composition, and WHR for fat distribution and cardiovascular risk.
